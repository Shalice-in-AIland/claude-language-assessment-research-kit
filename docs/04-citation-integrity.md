# Citation integrity — the rules Claude enforces (and you should too)

*The first question researchers ask about AI-assisted writing is the right one: **"how do we stop the AI inventing references?"** This page is the answer. The pipeline automates citation formatting; these rules govern citation **truth**. Claude follows them when it audits your library or runs the pre-submission integrity pass (the `citation-integrity` skill) — and they're worth following even with no AI in sight, because every failure mode below predates AI.*

**The AI-era ground rule: an AI may *format* and *check* citations — it may never *supply* one.** Any reference an AI (or a colleague, or your memory) suggests enters your draft only after it's verified as a real record in *your* Zotero library. If a claim needs support and no verified, on-point source exists, write **"[source needed]"** and move on — never let anything fill the gap from memory.

## The rules
*Sixteen rules in four groups, ordered by how badly things go wrong when the rule is broken: first the reference itself, then how you cite it, then the record-keeping, then declarations and the final gate.*

### A. The reference itself is real and right
1. **Never fabricate — and never mis-attach.** Every reference (authors, title, year, venue, DOI, pages) comes from a real record, **and** it must actually support the specific claim it's attached to. This bars two failures at once: an invented-but-plausible reference (still fabrication, however real it looks), and a *real* reference pinned to a claim it doesn't support.
2. **No "Frankenstein" references.** Never combine fields from different sources into one entry — one paper's authors with another's title or DOI. Every field of an entry comes from the *same single record*; if two records blur together, stop and flag both.
3. **Every reference resolves.** Each entry carries a DOI or stable URL that actually opens to the same author/title/year you cite. No verifiable identifier → flag it.
4. **Cite the version you actually used.** Editions, reprints, and preprint-vs-published versions differ. Check the copyright page / version of record and make the year and edition match *the copy you read* — a 2007 reprint of a 2000 book is cited as 2000; an advance-access PDF is cited as its version of record once one exists; a 2nd edition is not interchangeable with the 1st.

### B. The citing is honest scholarship
5. **Unread = not citable.** A paper gets cited only after someone on your team has *actually read it* — not just its abstract in a search result. Keep any simple reading log: a Zotero tag ("read"), a coloured flag, or a column in whatever spreadsheet you track literature in. If you know the work only through another paper's summary, either read the original or cite it honestly as a secondary source ("as cited in…", and list the source you actually read).
6. **Cite the original, not a stand-in.** Point to the primary source whenever possible: the study itself rather than a review that mentions it (cite reviews *as* reviews), and the published article rather than its abstract. If only the abstract or a summary was available to you, that's a rule-5 situation — say so.
7. **Weak evidence never carries weight alone.** A methodologically weak study (tiny sample, no reliability reported, opaque methods) may be cited for context or breadth — but not as the load-bearing support for a claim, and the caveat travels with the citation ("though based on n = 12…"). Grade your evidence, not just your formatting.
8. **Every citation earns its place.** A reference is there because it *contributes to the scholarly content* — never to inflate anyone's citation count. No padding with irrelevant sources, no excessive self-citation (cite your own work only where genuinely needed, e.g. research continuity), no citation swaps or favours. Publishers now actively screen for this ("citation manipulation") and penalise it.
9. **Quotes are verbatim, with a locator.** Direct quotes are copied exactly and carry a page/section number. Never approximate a page number; never present a paraphrase as a quote.

### C. The record is consistent — and never quietly altered
10. **One identity everywhere.** The same author string and year across Zotero, your citekey, the in-text citation, and the reference list; same-author-same-year disambiguated with a/b. (The pipeline handles this automatically if Zotero's record is right — which is why the record matters.)
11. **No orphans, either direction.** Every in-text citation has exactly one reference-list entry and vice versa. The pipeline enforces this at render time (`(key?)` warnings) — read the warnings, and check once more before anything is submitted.
12. **One style, from the style file.** Citations and the reference list follow one style — APA 7th here by default, or whatever your target venue requires — and the *style file* (`style.csl`) does that formatting, never your hands. Hand-tweaked citations are where inconsistencies (and errors) creep back in.
13. **No silent corrections.** If a reference detail looks wrong, surface the discrepancy — don't quietly "fix" it. (Quiet fixing is how a correct reference becomes a wrong one.)

### D. Status declared, AI disclosed, and the final gate
14. **Status is declared — and checked (automatically).** Preprints are cited *as* preprints (clearly marked as not peer-reviewed). Before submission, **verify that no reference is retracted** — a retracted work may be cited only when discussing its retraction. This is automated: `starter-kit/check_retractions.py` checks every DOI in your library against the **Crossref API, which incorporates the human-verified Retraction Watch database** — run `python3 check_retractions.py library.bib` (or ask Claude), and hand-check only the DOI-less entries it lists (typically books). Widely-cited papers *do* get retracted; manual checking doesn't scale, this does. Avoid citing predatory/pseudo-journals.
15. **AI is a tool to disclose — never a source to cite.** An AI tool's output is never cited as a scholarly source (it merely reproduces — often inaccurately — primary sources of unknown quality). Most major journals now require declaring the use of AI-assisted tools at submission (typically: writing assistance in the acknowledgements; analysis uses in the methods). An AI is never an author, and the humans bear **full responsibility** for anything AI-assisted — very much including its citations, which is what the rest of this page operationalises.
16. **A final integrity pass, logged.** The capstone: before submission, every citation traced to a real library record + resolving DOI, orphan check both ways, quotes/pages verified, retraction check run, disclosure written. Ask Claude to run it (it reports ✓ verified / ⚠ flagged and never silently repairs) — and note the date it was done.

## Why so strict?

Because formatting errors embarrass, but **integrity errors mislead** — and they compound: a fabricated or Frankenstein reference, once published, gets cited onward by readers who trusted you. These rules track what ethics bodies and publishers formally require (COPE on citation manipulation; ICMJE on references, retractions, preprints, and AI disclosure; publisher integrity policies). The pipeline makes group C nearly automatic (rules 10–12); Claude's audits cover group A and rules 9, 13, and 16 mechanically and remind you of 14–15 (retraction checks and disclosure can't be read off a .bib file); group B — actually reading, choosing originals, weighing evidence, and citing with judgement — is the part no tool can do, which is exactly why it's written down.
