# Your prose, polished — five dimensions, your voice kept

*The other guides manage what your field says (docs/07) and what it will say about your paper (docs/08); this page is about the sentences themselves. The `writing-polish` skill refines **your own prose** — built for multilingual and early-career academics writing to the field's standards — without ever becoming the author. Language polish is the one AI use most publishers explicitly permit; the discipline here is doing it so your voice, your numbers, and your claims stay exactly yours.*

> **Who does what.** Your part is the writing and every decision about it: you accept or reject each style proposal, one at a time, and you make the AI-use declaration your venue asks for. Claude's part: correcting unambiguous mechanical errors (with a change log so you learn the pattern), proposing everything stylistic with a one-line reason each, and never touching the untouchables.

## Think of it as…

A **professional copyeditor with a rulebook**. The rulebook has two speeds and a hard preserve-list:

- **Accuracy** (grammar, articles, prepositions, collocations, mechanics) is **corrected directly** — unambiguous errors don't deserve one-at-a-time approvals — and reported in a concise change log (original → corrected). Anything where a fix could shift meaning arrives instead as a tracked-changes-style suggestion for you to decide.
- **Everything stylistic is a numbered proposal** — original → proposed → the reason — accepted or rejected by you, one at a time. The reason is the teaching: the goal is that you need the skill less each time.
- **The preserve-list is never waived:** numbers and statistics, citations and the reference list, direct quotes, technical terms, meaning, and how strongly a claim is made. A "polish" that firmed up a claim or moved a number would be an error — the skill checks mechanically that none of it changed.

## One move

**Say: _"Polish this section for submission to ⟨journal⟩"_** (or just "polish this paragraph"). Claude asks only what the text doesn't answer — venue, section type — then works through the five dimensions:

1. **Accuracy** — the language itself, corrected with the change log.
2. **Clarity & readability** — one idea per sentence; especially *clusters* of long, dense sentences (one is a style choice; three in a row is a wall).
3. **Formality** — colloquialisms, contractions, vague quantifiers — while never over-formalizing: "utilize" is fine where it earns its place; the failure is choosing it *because* it sounds formal when "use" says the same thing.
4. **Register & conventions** — the field's objective-and-cordial register; topic sentences, cohesion, tense conventions by section.
5. **Stance** — hedges, boosters, self-mention: appropriateness edits proposed freely; anything that would strengthen or weaken the claim itself is flagged for your judgement, never applied.

**You should see:** your corrected text, the change log, numbered proposals grouped by dimension — and a closing **recurring-patterns note** (at most three) naming your personal patterns and pointing each at a free practice resource (Academic Phrasebank for stance, Purdue OWL for accuracy, UEFAP for style). That note is your takeaway checklist.

## The habits that keep it honest

1. **Accept proposals one at a time** — the skill deliberately never delivers a silently rewritten draft; a clean full version is assembled only when you ask, from your accepted changes and nothing else.
2. **Read the change log** — it's a lesson in your own patterns, not just a receipt.
3. **The declaration is yours** — most journals permit language polish and still expect it declared; check the venue's policy and include what it asks.
4. **Keep the outputs with the draft** — they quote your unpublished writing; they belong beside the manuscript, never in a public folder.
