# Evaluation criteria by article type

**Where these come from.** The backbone is **Elsevier's structured peer review prompts** — the section-by-section questions many Elsevier journals in this field (e.g. *Assessing Writing*, *System*, *Journal of Second Language Writing*, *Journal of English for Academic Purposes*) put on the reviewer's form, organized by the same two article types Elsevier uses: **original research** and **review article**. Folded in are the criteria the field's other major venues state to their reviewers or in their scope pages (quantitative reporting bars, theory–method expectations, scope gates), and the journal-distributed synthesis instruments (e.g. the SMART checklist — Chong 2025, doi:10.1080/29984475.2025.2456880). The venue-by-venue record these are drawn from is `journal-reviewer-guides.md` — nothing here is invented; every item traces to publisher- or journal-provided guidance.

The Elsevier prompts were written with biomedical research in mind; each item below is stated in this field's terms so the evaluation never asks a language-assessment paper for a patient population. **Every item is a Step-1 ledger check**: it produces a page-anchored observation and, where numeric, a PASS / FAIL / CANNOT CHECK entry — never a rewrite.

## Original research

Work section by section — these are the questions the paper's reviewers will be asked to answer about it:

- **Scope and article-profile fit** — the first gate is the desk, not the reviewer: several venues in this field state hard scope bars (a required data-and-outcomes focus, exclusion of "just surveys" or pilot studies, a stated preference for certain contributions to the journal's mission). Check the manuscript against the target venue's declared scope and prioritized profile (its section in `journal-reviewer-guides.md`) before evaluating anything else.
- **Title** — accurately and sufficiently describes the content; discoverable by the readers who need it; where the field's convention expects it, the study type is visible in the title.
- **Abstract** — reflects *all* essential aspects of the study, **including all major results and limitations**. Ledger check: every number in the abstract against the results tables; every limitation claimed against the limitations actually stated.
- **Introduction** — background and literature current and appropriate; the rationale clearly explained; **primary (and any secondary) objectives explicitly stated at the end of the introduction** — reviewers are told to look for them there. The venues raise the bar beyond currency: the literature review should be *critical* — a competent, evaluative engagement that the contribution claim rests on, not a catalogue — and the study should **explicitly build on previous research in the field's own venues**, not arrive parallel to it.
- **Theory–method link (and technology rationale)** — the method should be visibly *informed by* the theoretical framing, not adjacent to it (journals in this field state this as a criterion: "a rigorous research method informed by a strong theoretical underpinning"). Where a technology or AI system is central to the study, check for **a clear logic behind using this technology** — why this tool for this construct, beyond availability.
- **Methods: design** — described in enough detail to replicate, and the design **identified by name**: in this field that means labels like quasi-experimental with intact classes, pre/post with comparison group, cross-sectional survey, correlational/validation study, many-facet rater design, corpus-based, single-group repeated measures, mixed-methods sequential, replication. Then the load-bearing check: **can this design support the claim being made?** Intact-class quasi-experiments are the field's norm and acceptable — but the paper must not then talk as if it randomized.
- **Methods: participants and sample size** — who, described usefully: proficiency level *and how it was established* (an institutional label is not a measure), L1 background, context, recruitment and assignment, attrition. Sample size **justified** (power analysis or other reasoning), and noted at *every* level — this field's N is multi-level: participants, raters, texts/items, ratings per text. Papers routinely report only the first.
- **Methods: comparison conditions** — where there is a comparison group: what it actually received (described well enough that the contrast means something — "business as usual" is not a description), whether time-on-task was equated, who taught and who rated which group.
- **Methods: statistics** — analyses appropriately described: controls, sampling, and the reporting triad (p-values, confidence intervals, effect sizes). The field's quantitative-facing venues state explicit bars worth checking against any target journal: the **sampling process is checked for suitability** as a matter of course; **effect sizes are reported always — including for non-significant results and post-hoc comparisons**; descriptive-only results need an explicit context-only framing or inferential support; and where the venue names a reporting standard (e.g. the APA Journal Article Reporting Standards), the paper follows it.
- **Results** — presentation appropriate: do the tables and figures chosen best show the findings? Are sub-analyses missing that the claims need (CIs, effect sizes, sensitivity analyses)?
- **Practical significance** — the field's version of the reviewer form's "minimal clinically important difference" question: is the observed difference *meaningful on this scale*, judged against something — the test's SEM, a band boundary, a published benchmark? A 2-point gain on a 100-point rubric can be statistically detectable and educationally trivial. Almost no paper answers this; whenever effect claims are made, it is a fair and useful finding.
- **Discussion** — interpretation and conclusions **supported by the data and the study design** (descriptive ≠ normative, correlational ≠ causal); limitations genuinely engaged — theory, methods, and argument, not a ritual paragraph.
- **Implications beyond the local** — two expectations journals in this field state outright: a **visible practical application** alongside the theoretical base, and findings framed to **transcend the solely local aspect of the research context** — what does this mean outside the authors' own program? Check both are present and *proportionate to the evidence* (the practical implications of a small correlational study are suggestions, not prescriptions).
- **References** — the most up-to-date and relevant for the topic; ledger-check any that look wrong (the citation-integrity skill does this systematically). Preprints: any superseded by a version of record are updated to it; the rest are clearly labelled as preprints; and a load-bearing warrant resting on a non-peer-reviewed source is a finding — reviewers probe these whether or not the venue states a policy.
- **Ethics and integrity** — approval and informed consent; consent for learner texts used as data; anonymization of student work; in AI-related studies, *what was sent to which commercial model and whether participants consented to that*; instrument and data availability.
- **Overall** — is the research novel, or important in another declared way, for this journal and field? Any integrity concerns to raise?

## Research synthesis (review article)

The reviewer-form questions for the review article type, in field terms:

- **The venue gate, first** — several journals in this field restrict syntheses: some **proposal-gate review articles** (unsolicited ones are rejected outright), others exclude systematic reviews from their scope entirely. Before evaluating anything, check the target venue accepts this article type through the door the author plans to use (`journal-reviewer-guides.md`).
- **Rationale** — the need for *this* review, *now*, clearly justified — not just "no review exists."
- **Coverage** — comprehensive on the field's development and honest about future directions; the most relevant and recent advances discussed. Run the **parochialism check**: only one national or institutional tradition? Only Anglophone contexts? Only the last five years? Only work congenial to the authors' position? Name what's missing by *topic* (never invent specific citations to fill a gap).
- **Method transparency and reproducibility** — search strategies, inclusion/exclusion criteria, quality appraisal, and summary methods reported in enough detail to reproduce. "Risk of bias" translates here to **study-quality coding**: was there a scheme, was any double coding done and with what agreement, were primary studies appraised at all or just summarized?
- **The reporting-standard boundary** — for systematic reviews and meta-analyses it is entirely fair to require the full apparatus (flow accounting, databases, date ranges, search strings, criteria — PRISMA or the venue's named standard). For a *narrative or conceptual* review, demanding PRISMA is a category error: ask instead for transparency about scope and selection.
- **Statistics (meta-analyses)** — effect-size metric appropriate and justified; dependency among effect sizes handled; fixed vs random effects justified; heterogeneity reported *and interpreted*; publication bias assessed; moderator analyses pre-specified rather than fished. Where the analysis outruns the evaluation, say a statistician's pass is warranted. (The process-level version of these checks — including the promise audit and the artifact check — is the synthesis module in SKILL.md; run both.)
- **Structure and reporting** — organization, flow, subheadings, and alignment with whichever reporting standard applies.
- **Toolchain and availability** — expectations the synthesis-focused venues now state: **software and automation disclosed** (search tools, screening platforms, and any AI assistance in screening or coding); search strings, extraction instruments, and data available or their absence explained; included studies **marked in the reference list** where the venue's convention asks (e.g. asterisks); author-contribution statements (e.g. CRediT) where required.
- **Language quality** — comment only where clarity genuinely impedes understanding, with examples; the appropriate recommendation is professional copyediting, never "a native speaker." Many authors in this field are multilingual writers, and the journals publish on exactly that.
- **Journal-distributed checklists** — where the target venue ships its own synthesis instrument, run its items as an extra ledger section and tell the author to self-assess against it before submitting: e.g. the **SMART checklist** (Systematicity, Transparency, Accessibility, Reflexivity — with distinctives like registration/protocol + reported deviations, a minimum-three-databases rule with rationale, and documented process issues) or **Language Testing's Guidelines for Systematic Reviews**. Which venues ship what: `journal-reviewer-guides.md`.
